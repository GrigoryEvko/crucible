## 1. Reading guide and methodology

This synthesis is the output of a deliberate research process. Crucible's design has been growing accretively for ~9 months; CLAUDE.md, the misc/ documents, the safety/* headers, and the L0-L17 layer model record what we've already committed to. The question this document answers: **what is missing**?

To find missing primitives we ran two coordinated waves of research-survey agents.

The **first wave (7 agents)** scanned ML-systems research itself — training algorithms and optimizers, sequence-modeling architectures, distributed training and communication, inference serving, numerical and quantization, GPU architecture exploitation, and compiler/autotuning. The output was a five-primitive shortlist (session opaque state, SM-level task graph IR, time-range scoped view, e-graph engine, staleness-tagged Cipher event) and the strong meta-observation that ~80% of "novel" ML-systems research reduces to compositions of primitives Crucible already has.

The **second wave (10 agents)** scanned adjacent fields — applied math (lattice/order/category theory, randomized linear algebra, stochastic processes/martingales), physics (thermodynamics of computation, non-equilibrium transport, renormalization/spin-glass), PL theory (CRDTs and BloomL, session types and async MPST, effect systems and graded types), and HPC (async PGAS, topology-aware collectives, exascale scheduling). The output is much richer: theorem-backed primitives Crucible can adopt with their proofs attached, several cross-field corroborations where independent disciplines converge on the same construct, and explicit rejection of physics-analogy work that does not transfer.

The methodology yielded ~50 candidate primitives in total. After applying two filters — (a) "is this expressible as composition of what Crucible already has?" and (b) "does it survive the bit-exact replay-determinism axiom?" — about ten survive as genuinely new architectural commitments. Five of those are corroborated by multiple independent fields. Those five are the strongest candidates and lead the document.

Each primitive section below follows the same structure: what the primitive **is** (algebraic shape, citation), what it **does** for Crucible (which existing layer it enters, which existing wrapper it composes with or replaces), what it **buys** (capability or simplification), and what it **costs** (engineering effort, risk, dependencies).

The document is opinionated. Some candidate primitives are demoted from earlier survey notes ("Tier 1") because closer reading reveals they fold into the foundational refactor in §2. Others are promoted because cross-field corroboration is stronger than any single agent flagged. The final ordering reflects integrated judgment, not survey ranking.

## 2. The foundational refactor — `Graded<Modality, Lattice, T>` algebra restoration

### 2.1 The observation

Crucible's `safety/` directory currently contains roughly twelve concrete wrapper templates:

```
Linear<T>             move-only exclusive ownership
Refined<Pred, T>      predicate-checked construction
Tagged<T, Source>     phantom provenance
Secret<T>             classified-by-default
Permission<Tag>       CSL linearity (move-only token, sizeof = 1)
SharedPermission<Tag> fractional CSL with atomic refcount
Session<Proto>        type-state binary or MPST protocol
ScopedView<...>       lifetime-bounded borrow
Machine<States>       type-indexed state machine
Monotonic<T>          advance-only with comparison
AppendOnly<T>         no-erase / no-resize
ConstantTime<T>       branch-free timing-side-channel-resistant
WriteOnceNonNull<T*>  pointer-specialized single-set
FinalBy<T> / NotInherited<T>   structural non-extensibility
```

Each was extracted, in isolation, from the **graded dependent type theory** tradition (Abadi-Plotkin information-flow modalities; Atkey-Brady-McBride quantitative type theory; Orchard-Liepelt-Eades graded comonad/monad pairing; Abel-Danielsson-Eriksson graded modal dependent type theory). The extractions were correct in spirit but performed atomically — each wrapper became its own template with hand-rolled rules, hand-rolled composition behavior, hand-rolled equational reasoning.

The result works (~12K lines of safety code, ~1300 Lean theorems, zero `sorry`), but the underlying algebraic structure is invisible. Composing `Secret<Tagged<T, source::FromUser>>` works because we wrote it to work, not because it is a consequence of the modality-and-lattice algebra.

The papers from the second-wave survey — particularly **Orchard, Liepelt, Eades, *Graded Modal Types for Integrity and Confidentiality*** ([arXiv:2309.04324](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.04324)) and **Abel, Danielsson, Eriksson, *A Graded Modal Dependent Type Theory with a Universe and Erasure, Formalized*** ([ACM DL 3607862](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3607862)) — formalize the algebra Crucible's wrappers were already using. Restoring that algebra is the foundational refactor of this synthesis.

### 2.2 The construction

A graded modality `□_r A` parameterized by:

- a **modality form** ∈ `{Comonad, RelativeMonad, Absolute, Relative}` (information flowing in or out, with the lattice attached or external)
- a **lattice or semiring** `(R, ⊑, ⊕, ⊗, e_⊕, e_⊗)` whose elements are the grades
- the underlying type `A`

with operations `unit_R(A) → □_e_⊕ A`, `extract: □_r A → A` (comonad), `join_R: □_r □_s A → □_{r⊕s} A`, `weaken: □_r A → □_s A` when `r ⊑ s`, and `bind`-like operations for monads and relative monads.

Encoded in C++26:

```cpp
template <ModalityKind M, Lattice L, typename T>
class Graded {
    [[no_unique_address]] T inner_;
public:
    // Construction from raw value with lattice element attached
    template <auto r> static consteval Graded mk(T x) requires (L::is_initial(r));

    // Lattice operations are consteval over std::meta::info
    template <auto s> consteval Graded<M, L, T> weaken() const
        requires (L::leq(grade_v<this>, s));

    template <auto s>
    consteval Graded<M, L, T> compose(Graded<M, L, T> other) const
        -> Graded<M, L, T_with_grade<L::join(r, s)>>;

    // Counit / unit depending on M
    constexpr T extract() const requires (M == Comonad);
    static constexpr Graded inject(T x) requires (M == RelativeMonad);
};
```

The lattice element flows through the type system at compile time (via reflection P2996, expansion statements P1306, structured-bindings packs P1061, all of which Crucible already uses for reflection-driven hashing). The runtime representation is whatever `T` was — phantom wrapping with empty-base optimization confirms `sizeof(Graded<M, L, T>) == sizeof(T)` under `-O3`.

### 2.3 The instantiations — existing wrappers as type aliases

Once `Graded<>` lands, every existing wrapper becomes a type alias over a specific (modality, lattice) pair:

```cpp
// QTT linearity semiring (Atkey 2018, Idris 2)
using QTT = Semiring<{0, 1, ω}, +, ·, 0, 1>;
template <typename T> using Linear = Graded<Absolute, QTT::At<1>, T>;

// Boolean lattice on a predicate
template <typename Pred, typename T>
using Refined = Graded<Absolute, BoolLattice<Pred>, T>;

// Information-flow comonad over a confidentiality lattice
template <typename T>
using Secret = Graded<Comonad, ConfLattice, T>;

// Information-flow relative monad over a trust/source lattice
template <typename T, typename Source>
using Tagged = Graded<RelativeMonad, TrustLattice<Source>, T>;

// Fractional CSL permission (rational [0,1] semiring)
template <typename Tag>
using SharedPermission = Graded<Absolute, FractionalLattice, Tag>;

// Monotonic on an arbitrary partial order
template <typename T, typename Cmp = std::less<T>>
using Monotonic = Graded<Absolute, MonotoneLattice<T, Cmp>, T>;

// Append-only on the prefix lattice of sequences
template <typename T>
using AppendOnly = Graded<Absolute, SeqPrefixLattice, T>;
```

The existing API is preserved exactly — public users see `Secret<T>` and `Tagged<T, Source>` as before. The internals collapse to one template plus ~10 lattice definitions plus ~12 type aliases. The safety directory shrinks roughly 60%.

### 2.4 The capabilities unlocked — new instantiations

The point of the refactor is not the line-count reduction; it is what falls out for free. Once `Graded<>` is the algebra, new lattices instantiate it without writing new wrapper templates. The following primitives — flagged variously by other surveys as gaps — become **lattice instantiations** rather than new code:

```cpp
// Async-first training: staleness as a grade
//   ([lattice survey #4: graded modal types for staleness])
//   ([martingales survey #2: ASGD delay-adaptive stepsize bound])
//   ([thermo survey #2: ASGD SDDE coupling])
using StalenessSemiring = Semiring<{ℕ ∪ ∞}, min, +, ∞, 0>;

template <typename T>
using Stale = Graded<Absolute, StalenessSemiring, T>;
//   Stale<T> at grade τ means "value of type T computed under bounded staleness τ"
//   Compose: Stale<T> at τ1, Stale<U> at τ2 yields a result at τ1 + τ2

// Compute-budget grades
//   ([effect-systems survey #2: Resource-Bounded Type Theory, arXiv:2512.06952])
using LatencyBudget = Lattice<microseconds, ≤>;
using EnergyBudget  = Lattice<joules, ≤>;
using BitsBudget    = Lattice<bits, ≤>;
using PeakBytes     = Lattice<bytes, ≤>;

template <auto budget, typename T>
using Budgeted = Graded<Absolute, ProductLattice<LatencyBudget, EnergyBudget, BitsBudget>, T>;
//   Budgeted<{1ms, 1kJ, 100MB}, ResultTensor> means "computed within (1ms, 1kJ, 100MB)"
//   Augur's measurements become compile-time bounds checked at the boundary

// Per-axis consistency tier as a grade
//   ([CRDT survey #3: Peepco batch-based per-axis consistency, OOPSLA 2025])
enum class Consistency : uint8_t { Strong, BoundedStaleness, CausalPrefix, ReadYourWrites, Eventual };
using ConsistencyLattice = Lattice<Consistency, ⊑>;
//   per-parallelism-axis level: {TP→Strong, DP→Eventual, ...}
//   feeds BatchPolicy<Axis, Level> in §5
```

That gives Crucible roughly twenty lattice instantiations across the codebase: confidentiality, trust, predicate-Boolean, linearity {0,1,ω}, fractional permission ℚ[0,1], monotonicity, sequence prefix, staleness (ℕ∪∞, min, +), latency budget, energy budget, bits budget, peak-bytes budget, consistency, plus context-specific ones.

### 2.5 Why this is load-bearing

The refactor is foundational because **every other primitive in this document either is a `Graded<L, T>` instantiation or composes with one**. Section 4 (`BatchPolicy<Axis, Level>`) needs a consistency lattice; §6 (Augur dashboard) needs resource lattices; §7 (ASGD admission gate) needs the staleness semiring; §13 (MDM-OC) is a `Tagged<OrthogonalDelta<...>, T>` which is a `Graded` instance; §14 (TimelineSlice) composes with `Refined ⊂ Graded`. Building the rest before the foundation forces re-implementations later.

The refactor also changes Crucible's relationship to its own Lean proofs. The `lean/Crucible/` directory currently has 36 modules and ~1300 theorems split across the wrappers. Targeting a single graded calculus lets the proofs compose: prove a property of `Graded<M, L, T>` once, get it for every alias.

### 2.6 Cost and risk

Engineering cost: ~3-5K LoC for the `Graded<>` template plus ~10 lattice definitions plus the ~12 type aliases that preserve the existing API. The risk is template-instantiation explosion if naive — mitigated by judicious `consteval` and by the public API staying identical, so old call sites compile unchanged.

Time risk: this is a Phase 2a refactor. Doing it before the rest of the work in this document is non-negotiable. Building, say, `BatchPolicy` first and then refactoring would require rewriting `BatchPolicy`'s level type. Building `Stale<T>` first would require deleting it when `Graded<StalenessSemiring, T>` lands.

## 3. Effect rows — replacing `fx::*` capability tokens with Met(X)

### 3.1 The current state

Crucible's effect tokens — `fx::Alloc`, `fx::IO`, `fx::Block`, `fx::Bg`, `fx::Init`, `fx::Test` — are zero-size phantom structs passed by value. They are effectively a hand-rolled effect row, encoded as a heterogeneous template parameter pack. Composition is by `static_assert(has<fx::Bg, Caps...>)` patterns scattered through the codebase. This works but is rigid: rows cannot be quantified over, modalities are not first-class, and the effect type is fused with the function arrow rather than living independently.

### 3.2 The Met(X) construction

**Tang & Lindley, *Rows and Capabilities as Modal Effects* (POPL 2026, [arXiv:2507.10301](https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.10301))** present a System-F-style calculus parameterized by an abstract effect structure X. A judgement `Γ ⊢ M : A @ E` says "term M has type A at mode E", where E is a row. Two modalities — absolute `[E]` and relative `⟨L|D⟩` — encode classical effect-type approaches uniformly (Koka-style row polymorphism, Scala 3 capture sets, Eff-style algebraic-effect handlers).

In C++26 encoding, the row is a `consteval` `std::meta::info` set and rows compose by set union. Subrow inclusion is `consteval`. The wrapper is:

```cpp
template <auto Row, typename T>
class Computation {
    T inner_;
public:
    constexpr T extract() const && requires (Row.empty());

    template <auto Cap>
    static constexpr Computation lift(T x) requires (Row.contains(Cap));

    // Row weakening — a more-permissive caller can use a less-permissive computation
    template <auto Row2>
    constexpr Computation<Row2, T> weaken() const requires (Subrow<Row, Row2>);

    // Bind / map / sequence as algebraic effect handlers
    template <auto Row2, typename U>
    constexpr Computation<Row | Row2, U>
        then(Computation<Row2, U>(*k)(T)) const;
};

template <auto Caps, typename T>
using fx::Bg<T> = Computation<Row<{Effect::Bg}>, T>;
```

The `static_assert(has<fx::Bg, Caps...>)` pattern collapses to `requires Subrow<Caps, Row<{Effect::Bg}>>`, which the compiler enforces uniformly across the codebase.

### 3.3 What it buys

- **Composable effects.** A function returning `Computation<{Bg, Alloc}, T>` can be passed as a value, stored in a struct, mapped over without the effect set fragmenting the call signature.
- **Single rule for capability propagation.** Subrow inclusion replaces ~40 `static_assert` sites that hand-encode the same monotonicity.
- **Quantification.** Generic code can be parametric over the effect row, as `template <auto R, typename T> void log(Computation<R, T>)` — currently impossible because `fx::*` is a heterogeneous type-list, not a value.
- **Modality decoupling.** Met(X)'s absolute vs. relative modalities map naturally to Crucible's distinction between "this function MAY allocate" and "this function MUST allocate from a specific arena" — making the Arena vs. heap distinction part of the type rather than a comment.

### 3.4 Migration

Type aliases preserve every public effect-token name. Old code reads:

```cpp
void compile_kernel(fx::Bg, Arena&, KernelNode const&);
```

New code reads identically (because `fx::Bg` is a type alias) but the underlying machinery uses Met(X). The migration is fully internal to the safety/Effects.h header.

## 4. Async-safe bit-exact replay — `Cipher::ReplayLog<Ordering>`

### 4.1 The problem

Crucible's central invariant is **DetSafe**: training state at step T is a pure function of `(weights_T, optimizer_T, data_cursor_T, seed)`. This invariant is established across the system — Philox counter-RNG instead of stateful PRNG, content-addressed static memory plans, pinned reduction topology, four NumericalRecipe determinism tiers — and validated by the `bit_exact_replay_invariant` and `cross_vendor_step_invariant` CI tests (CRUCIBLE.md §10.8).

The invariant holds for **synchronous** training. It breaks the moment the system goes asynchronous. Decoupled DiLoCo, INTELLECT-2, HALoS, DisTrO/Psyche — every async-first training scheme operates in a regime where workers commit gradients out of step order, with vector clocks and grace windows replacing the strict barrier. The order in which two pseudo-gradients of equal staleness arrive at the aggregator depends on network jitter, which depends on real-time conditions. Replay cannot reproduce that order from `(weights, seed, data_cursor)` alone.

Three independent surveys converged on the answer.

### 4.2 The construction

The HPC tradition has solved this exact problem for decades. **MPIWIZ** (subgroup reproducible replay of MPI applications, [Tsinghua PACMAN PDF](https://pacman.cs.tsinghua.edu.cn/papers_cwg/mpiwiz.pdf)) and the **actor-language record-replay line** ([arXiv:1805.06267](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1805.06267)) record the *control-flow ordering* of nondeterministic events — message arrivals, wildcard matches, async I/O completions — rather than the message data itself. The data is reconstructible from the deterministic execution given the order; the order is not. Recording only the order is logarithmic in event count, not linear in data volume.

For Crucible the ordering log is a sequence of events:

```cpp
struct OrderingEvent {
    StepId         step;        // monotone step index
    SourceUuid     worker;      // who committed
    VectorClock    vc;          // happens-before
    StalenessTag   tau;         // age (in outer-steps) of the gradient at admission
    EventKind      kind;        // ASYNC_GRAD_ARRIVED | SPOT_EVICTED | PEER_RECONNECTED | RESHARD_EPOCH_BUMP
    ContentHash    payload_hash;// what was committed (from the bit-exact data tier)
};

class Cipher::ReplayLog {
public:
    // ~16 bytes per event after compression; small relative to gradient data
    void record(OrderingEvent ev);
    std::span<const OrderingEvent> replay_window(StepId from, StepId to) const;
    // Replay walks events in recorded order and re-applies them with the
    // same admission-policy decisions; the data tier is content-addressed
    // so the actual numerics are reproduced bit-exactly.
};
```

The recording is on the same Cipher infrastructure as weight snapshots, but the per-event size is 4-5 orders of magnitude smaller than a gradient batch. A 1000-step async-DiLoCo run generates ~10⁵-10⁶ ordering events and roughly 1.6-16 MB of replay log, easily fitting in the warm tier.

### 4.3 Composition with the staleness type

The ordering log composes with `Stale<T>` from §2.4. Each event carries the staleness tag at which it was admitted, and the replay logic re-applies events with the same admission policy — so the type-level staleness grade and the run-time replay log together suffice to reproduce the same admission decisions. **This composition is what extends DetSafe from synchronous to asynchronous training.** Without the ordering log, async DiLoCo cannot pass `bit_exact_replay_invariant`. With it, the test extends to async without modification.

### 4.4 Lean proof obligation

The async-extended DetSafe is a natural Lean proof target:

```
theorem async_replay_bit_exact :
  ∀ (program : DAG) (seed : Seed) (events : ReplayLog),
    ∀ (run1 run2 : ExecutionTrace),
      run1.replays_with(events) ∧ run2.replays_with(events)
      → run1.weights = run2.weights
```

This sits naturally in `lean/Crucible/PermissionFlow.lean` or a new `AsyncFlow.lean`, alongside the existing safety axioms.

### 4.5 Cost

Engineering cost: ~3-5 KLoC for the log writer/reader, integration with existing Cipher tiers, and admission-policy hooks. The Lean proof is a research-tier task in Phase 5 of the safety-integration plan.

## 5. Per-axis consistency lattice — `BatchPolicy<Axis, Level>` on ExecutionPlan

### 5.1 The user-facing question

Crucible already supports 5D parallelism (TP × DP × PP × EP × CP) with a static partition produced by Forge Phase K. The natural follow-up question: **can different axes of the partition operate at different consistency tiers?** TP stays strictly synchronous because intra-tensor partial sums must rejoin within a step; DP can tolerate bounded-staleness async because gradient aggregation has well-known convergence guarantees under bounded delay; PP needs causal-prefix because forward-pass activations must precede their consumption; EP can be `read-your-writes`; CP varies.

If the answer is yes, the system gets dramatically more flexible and dramatically more bandwidth-efficient. If the answer is no, async-first training stays a special-case branch of code rather than a first-class axis.

### 5.2 The Peepco answer

**Peepco — Batch-Based Consistency Optimization** (OOPSLA 2025, [ACM DL 3720513](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3720513), [MIT DSpace PDF](https://dspace.mit.edu/bitstream/handle/1721.1/164690/3720513.pdf)) gives the framework. Given a *bounded* batch of operations, Peepco statically infers the weakest consistency level each operation needs from a satisfiability check on integrity predicates. Consistency is parameterized at runtime: the protocol is reconfigured per op rather than fixed cluster-wide.

The bounded-batch assumption is exactly what an ExecutionPlan provides — one training step is a static, bounded sequence of operations. The Peepco static-inference algorithm runs at Phase J (EMIT) when the plan is composed; the result is a `BatchPolicy<Axis, Level>` annotation:

```cpp
struct BatchPolicy {
    // Per-parallel-axis consistency level chosen by static inference
    Consistency tp = Consistency::Strong;
    Consistency dp = Consistency::Eventual;       // or BoundedStaleness(K)
    Consistency pp = Consistency::CausalPrefix;
    Consistency ep = Consistency::ReadYourWrites;
    Consistency cp = Consistency::Strong;
};

class ExecutionPlan {
    // ... existing fields
    BatchPolicy policy;
};
```

The `Consistency` type from §2.4 is itself a graded lattice — so `BatchPolicy` composes with the grading discipline. Per-axis tiers can be tightened or loosened per-plan via Peepco's static inference, and the runtime can be **looser than the inferred bound** without affecting correctness (only performance).

### 5.3 What it buys

- Production deployments can run TP synchronously over NVLink while DP runs Decoupled-DiLoCo-style across geographic regions with bounded staleness, in the same training run, with the same DAG, under the same NumericalRecipe — without the user manually picking tiers.
- New axes can be added (silicon-role partition, microbatch-size axis) and Peepco's inference adapts automatically.
- Observability becomes trivial: Augur reports per-axis consistency tier observed vs. tier required, surfacing op-level inefficiencies.

### 5.4 Composition with `ChainEdge`

ChainEdge semaphores currently sequence plans synchronously by their `expected_value` and `signal_value` fields. With BatchPolicy, the semaphore fields are reparameterized per consistency level: `Strong` waits exactly, `BoundedStaleness(K)` waits with a slack window of K outer-steps, `CausalPrefix` waits only for happens-before predecessors, etc. The reparameterization is at submit-time; pushbuffer composition is unchanged.

## 6. Randomized linear algebra as a first-class kernel kind — `SKETCH`

### 6.1 The opportunity

Randomized numerical linear algebra (RNLA) — sketching, structured random projections, randomized SVD, randomized trace estimation — has been a research staple since the 2010s. The 2025 development that matters for Crucible is **standardization with deterministic seed plumbing**: the **RandBLAS** standard ([UC Berkeley EECS-2025-58](https://www2.eecs.berkeley.edu/Pubs/TechRpts/2025/EECS-2025-58.pdf), [arXiv:2302.11474](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.11474), [GitHub repo](https://github.com/BallisticLA/RandBLAS)) defines counter-based-PRNG-seeded sketching operators where every entry of the sketch matrix is `Philox(counter=(row, col, stream), key=recipe_seed)`. There is no shared RNG state, every worker computes its slice independently, and the output is **bit-exact across chips and shards**.

This is exactly Crucible's existing Philox discipline applied to structural randomized algebra. Adopting it is essentially free.

### 6.2 The construction

A new KernelKind in the IR002 taxonomy (extending FORGE.md §18.1 from 22 kinds to 23):

```cpp
enum class KernelKind : uint8_t {
    // ... existing kinds
    SKETCH,   // randomized linear-algebra sketch operator
};

struct SketchAttrs {
    SketchVariant variant;  // CountSketch | SRHT | SparseSign | Gaussian | KhatriRao
    const Expr* d_in;        // input dimension
    const Expr* d_sketch;    // sketched dimension
    ScalarType  in_dtype;
    ScalarType  sketch_dtype;

    // Deterministic seed derivation
    SketchSeedDerivation seed_source;  // RecipeScoped | OpScoped | GlobalCounter

    // Extension points (Khatri-Rao factor sizes if applicable)
    const ComputeBody* per_factor_body = nullptr;

    ExecutionAttrs exec;
};
```

The sketch operator becomes a regular KernelNode in the IR002 KernelGraph, lowered per-vendor by Mimic, MAP-Elites-tunable for tile shapes. The `seed_source` field controls reproducibility scope: `RecipeScoped` means every replay of the same recipe reproduces the same sketch matrix; `OpScoped` derives from the op's content hash; `GlobalCounter` derives from a per-run counter for variance reduction.

### 6.3 What it unlocks

Several capabilities that previously required custom kernels become regular Forge-emitted programs:

- **Hessian spectrum estimation** for L11 (Pearlmutter HvP + randomized SVD with bit-exact across chips)
- **K-FAC factor compression** for L9 (per-layer sketched preconditioner)
- **Trace estimation** for natural-gradient methods (Hutchinson variant with deterministic seed)
- **Low-rank weight quantization** (sketched factorization as an L10 architecture mutation)
- **Randomized attention** (sketched key/value projections for very-long-context inference)
- **Cross-Rank-1 Update Detection** (cheap monitoring for L14 continuous learning)

All are bit-exact across chips because the seed is content-addressed.

### 6.4 Khatri-Rao for tensor operations

The 2025 Khatri-Rao analysis ([arXiv:2507.23207](https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.23207)) closed the long-standing pessimism gap. Tight error bounds match empirical performance, and sketching cost drops from O(nd) to O(n√d) for naturally-factored tensors. Forge Phase TILE detects Tucker- or TT-compressible patterns (rank-detection at L15 Augur) and swaps dense sketch for KRP sketch via a `SKETCH_KHATRI_RAO` sub-variant.

## 7. The six-scalar Augur dashboard — replacing one heuristic with six theorems

### 7.1 The current state

Augur's continuous monitoring (CRUCIBLE.md §13, FORGE.md §17 Phase L) detects drift via a single signal: per-kernel cycle residual, with the rule "P95 > 10% for ≥100 consecutive samples triggers recalibration". The threshold is hand-picked. The signal aggregates many distinct failure modes into one indicator. When it fires, attribution requires manual investigation.

The cross-field survey produced six independent runtime-computable scalars, each backed by a theorem from a different field, each measuring a distinct property of the running system, each with a theorem-derived threshold. Together they replace the single heuristic with a principled per-cause dashboard.

### 7.2 The six scalars

**Scalar 1 — Sharp Matrix Empirical Bernstein confidence sequence** (martingales survey).

Wang, Waudby-Smith, Ramdas (NeurIPS 2025), *Sharp Matrix Empirical Bernstein Inequalities*, [arXiv:2411.09516](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.09516):

> For matrix-valued martingale-difference sequence (X_t) with λ_max(X_t) ≤ b a.s., letting S_n = Σ X_t and V_n = Σ X_t², for any α ∈ (0,1), simultaneously over all n ≥ 1:
> Pr[ λ_max(S_n) ≤ inf_{λ ∈ (0, 1/b)} (1/λ)(log(d/α) + λ²·λ_max(V_n)·ψ(λb)) ] ≥ 1 − α.

This is **anytime-valid** (any stopping time keeps the bound), **self-normalized** (uses realized V_n, not an a-priori variance proxy), and **tight** (no constant slack). It directly replaces the "P95 > 10% for 100 samples" heuristic with a confidence-sequence radius `ε_n` that gets sharper as evidence accumulates. The metric is `λ_max(S_n)` of the kernel-residual matrix; the threshold is `ε_n` computed on the fly. CipherEvent admission (§4) checks the inequality at every event.

**Scalar 2 — Wasserstein-2 dissipation ratio** (thermo survey).

[arXiv:2503.01200](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.01200), building on Van Vu & Saito (PRX 13:011013, 2023):

> Σ ≥ W₂(ρ_initial, ρ_final)² / (2·D·τ)

Finite-time entropy production is universally bounded below by Wasserstein-2 distance over protocol time. For training, ρ_initial and ρ_final are the gradient/activation distributions before and after a step; W₂ is computable cheaply via Augur's existing histograms; D is the kernel's effective diffusion coefficient. The ratio `(measured_joules · τ) / W₂²` quantifies how far the hardware is operating from the thermodynamically optimal protocol. Values >>1 flag kernels where a slower, lower-precision recipe would save energy without convergence harm.

**Scalar 3 — Distributed-estimation bits-floor ratio** (thermo survey).

[arXiv:2511.21015](https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.21015) and [arXiv:2506.05271](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2506.05271):

> For distributed mean-estimation of a bounded function to accuracy ε, communication is tightly Θ(R(f)/ε) bits per round.

Given the precision-budget calibrator's per-op ε (§10), no distributed all-reduce can use fewer than R(f)·log(1/ε)/ε bits per step without losing the accuracy guarantee. Augur's bandwidth metric becomes `bits_per_step / lower_bound`; ratios near 1 mean the current compressor is optimal; ratios >>1 expose communication slack. The ratio drives compressor selection (gradient quantization recipe choice) automatically.

**Scalar 4 — Per-layer Δ^G symmetry-break distance** (spin-glass survey).

Ziyin (2025), *Parameter Symmetry Potentially Unifies Deep Learning Theory*, [arXiv:2502.05300](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.05300):

> For each parameter symmetry group G (permutation of hidden units, sign flips, rescaling), define Δ^G(θ) = min_{g∈G} ‖θ − g·θ‖ / ‖θ‖. Empirically Δ^G > ~0.05-0.2 marks symmetry breaking — the moment the network commits to a feature-learning direction.

For the permutation group on hidden units this reduces to a linear-assignment problem on weight columns, O(h³) per layer, scheduled every ~1K steps on Augur's background thread. The per-layer distribution of Δ^G distinguishes lazy-NTK kernels from feature-learning kernels — making the regime visible at runtime. As a MAP-Elites behavior axis, "symmetry-break depth profile" bucketed into {flat, early-heavy, late-heavy, full} separates kernels that current MAP-Elites cells lump together.

**Scalar 5 — DMFT memory-kernel tail fraction** (spin-glass survey).

[Rigorous DMFT for SGD, SIAM JMDS 2025](https://epubs.siam.org/doi/abs/10.1137/23M1594388) and Montanari-Urbani [NeurIPS 2025](https://neurips.cc/virtual/2025/poster/118767):

> SGD dynamics in the high-dimensional limit reduce to a scalar effective equation with a memory kernel M(τ) coupling step t to past steps t-τ. Generalization is dominated by the short-τ part; overfitting by the long-τ tail. The "long-tail fraction" ∫_{τ>τ*} M / ∫M crossing ~0.3 predicts overfitting regime with measurable lead time over validation loss.

Compute: Augur maintains a rolling gradient-inner-product ring (~1024 entries, ~1KB/layer); the M(τ) covariance derives from it on the background thread. The metric is the integrated tail beyond τ* = 10·batch_step. **Crucially this works on training data alone** — no held-out validation set required, critical for continuous-learning scenarios where val-loss curves don't exist.

**Scalar 6 — NTK spectral tail exponent α** (spin-glass survey).

[Renormalization Group for DNNs, arXiv:2510.25553](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.25553):

> The empirical NTK spectrum λ_k ~ k^(-α). The exponent α determines learning-curve slope, feature-learning-vs-lazy assignment, and whether finite-width perturbations are relevant or irrelevant at the GP-like UV fixed point. Drift in α during training signals a phase-boundary crossing.

Compute: randomized SVD of a small sample-sample kernel block on a frozen probe batch (~1024 examples, Philox-seeded for replay-safety); fit log λ_k vs log k over the middle decade. Drift Δα > 0.1 triggers Meridian recalibration. More principled than loss-curve slope as a regime indicator.

### 7.3 The composition

All six are pure functions of `(θ_t, g_t, fixed_probe_batch)` and run on Augur's background thread without perturbing bit-exact replay. The probe batches are Philox-seeded for cross-run reproducibility. The composition with `Graded<>` from §2.4 is direct: each scalar's threshold is a `Budgeted<ResourceLattice, T>` bound; the dashboard query is a `Computation<{Bg}, AugurMetrics>` returning the six scalars at once.

The Augur tick:

```cpp
struct AugurMetrics {
    // Scalar 1 — Sharp MEB confidence sequence
    double meb_lambda_max;
    double meb_threshold;

    // Scalar 2 — Wasserstein dissipation ratio
    double wasserstein_ratio;       // (measured_joules · τ) / W₂²

    // Scalar 3 — Bits floor ratio
    double bits_per_step_ratio;     // measured / lower_bound

    // Scalar 4 — Per-layer symmetry break
    std::span<const double> delta_g;     // one entry per layer

    // Scalar 5 — DMFT memory tail
    double dmft_tail_fraction;

    // Scalar 6 — NTK spectral tail
    double ntk_alpha;
    double ntk_alpha_drift;
};

void augur_tick(fx::Bg, KeeperContext& ctx, AugurMetrics& out);
```

Each metric has its own remediation path documented in the recipe registry. When MEB fires, recalibrate the timing model. When Wasserstein fires, downgrade the precision recipe. When bits-floor fires, switch the compressor. When Δ^G crosses, log a feature-learning-regime transition. When DMFT tail crosses, trigger checkpoint pinning. When NTK α drifts, trigger Meridian recalibration.

### 7.4 What it buys

- **Six independent failure modes** become independently diagnosable instead of a single opaque alarm.
- **Theorem-derived thresholds** mean the alarm fires when something demonstrably wrong happens, not when a hand-picked constant trips.
- **Replay-safe**: every metric is deterministic from `(θ, g, probe_batch_seed)`.
- **Anytime validity** (from MEB and Doob-induced sequential testing, [arXiv:2501.03982](https://arxiv.org/html/2501.03982)) lets Augur monitor continuously without inflating Type-I error over many checks.

## 8. The ASGD admission gate — `η · λ_max(H) · τ ≤ critical`

### 8.1 The cross-corroborated finding

The single most significant cross-field corroboration in the entire survey: the criterion that controls async-SGD convergence is the product `η · λ_max(H) · τ` — learning rate times Hessian spectral norm times staleness. Mishchenko, Bach, Even, Woodworth ([arXiv:2206.07638](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2206.07638)) derive it from convergence theory; Yu, Chen, Poor ([arXiv:2406.11159](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.11159)) derive the same product from a Stochastic Delay Differential Equation perspective; the thermo survey independently arrived at it via the SDDE coupling between irreversibility and delay.

Three independent fields converging on the same product is the strongest possible signal. The product is the **right** quantity to gate on.

### 8.2 The gate

```cpp
struct AsyncAdmissionPolicy {
    double critical;          // theorem-derived threshold (tier-dependent)
    double current_eta;       // measured learning rate
    double lambda_max_H;      // estimated Hessian spectral norm (Pearlmutter HvP, sampled)
    StalenessTag tau;         // grade of the incoming event

    bool admit() const {
        return current_eta * lambda_max_H * tau.value <= critical;
    }
};
```

Each axis of the threshold:
- `current_eta` is per-recipe.
- `λ_max(H)` is estimated by Crucible's existing Hessian-vector-product machinery (L11), sampled at a rate of one outer step in 100. Cost is one extra forward-backward pass per sample, well within Augur's budget.
- `τ` is the staleness grade carried by the incoming CipherEvent, derived from its vector clock.
- `critical` is recipe-tier-dependent: BITEXACT_STRICT forbids any τ > 0; BITEXACT_TC allows τ in {0,1}; ORDERED allows τ up to a recipe-specific bound; UNORDERED relaxes fully.

When the inequality fires, the aggregator either rejects the event (forcing the worker to retry with fresher state) or downgrades the recipe tier for the affected partition. Both responses are deterministic and replay-safe.

### 8.3 Composition with `Graded<StalenessSemiring, T>`

The staleness tag τ is the grade of the event's `Stale<T>` type from §2.4. The admission gate is the boundary check at the point where `Stale<T>` enters the synchronous Cipher tier. The grade arithmetic (τ_combined = τ_event + τ_already_committed) tracks staleness compositionally; the gate fires on the combined grade.

### 8.4 Cost

Engineering cost: ~1-2 KLoC for the policy, plus integration with the existing Pearlmutter HvP machinery (already in L11). The threshold calibration is one experiment per supported model architecture × hardware tier, runs once at deploy time.

## 9. Silicon-role tagging for heterogeneous AFD fleets

### 9.1 The deployment reality

The NVIDIA GTC 2026 announcements — Rubin CPX (prefill GPU, 192 SMs on TSMC N3P, 128GB GDDR7, 2 TB/s, no NVLink switch, designed for compute-bound prefill) and Groq 3 LPX (256-LPU rack, 500MB SRAM/LPU at 150 TB/s, deterministic compiler-orchestrated execution, designed for SRAM-resident decode-FFN) — split a single fleet into three silicon classes. NVIDIA's Dynamo orchestrator routes prefill to Rubin GPUs, decode-attention to Rubin GPUs, decode-FFN to LPUs. The pattern is general: any sufficiently optimized inference stack will run different op classes on different silicon.

Crucible's existing TargetCaps assumes homogeneous silicon per vendor. To deploy on AFD-style fleets, the abstraction needs a role bitmap.

### 9.2 The construction

Add `SiliconRole` as a tag on TargetCaps (FORGE.md §21):

```cpp
enum class SiliconRole : uint16_t {

struct TargetCaps {
    // ... existing fields

};
```

### 9.3 The Phase K extension

The Z3 partitioner from FORGE.md §25.6 extends with two new variable families:

```
For each kernel k:
    role[k] ∈ SiliconRole
    chip[k] ∈ fleet_members  (subject to role[k] ∈ chip.roles)

Constraints (added):
    kv_affinity:   attention kernels for the same session land on the same chip class
                   across decode steps
    activation_xfer_cost[k1 → k2]: non-zero iff role[k1] ≠ role[k2]

Objective (added terms):
    + Σ activation_xfer_time[k1 → k2]

```

The result is a placement that respects silicon-role affinity, minimizes inter-role activation traffic, and keeps KV caches on the right silicon class.

Three properties distinguish a Crucible-native AFD from Dynamo:

1. **Cross-vendor.** Dynamo's AFD is locked to Rubin + Groq LPX. ## 10. The precision-budget calibrator — declarative tolerance

### 10.1 The current state

Every ML framework on the market asks the user to pick a static precision (FP32 / FP16 / BF16 / FP8) and applies it uniformly. The reality is that some layers tolerate FP8, others need FP32 accumulator, some inputs produce denormal-boundary activations, and error amplification through the network is per-op and per-batch-distribution. Static allowlists are wrong on both ends — too conservative for safe layers, too aggressive for sensitive ones.

Crucible has the machinery to do this correctly: the four-tier NumericalRecipe registry, MAP-Elites per-kernel archives keyed on (recipe, shape, target), DAG branches that swap atomically at iteration boundary, cross-vendor numerics CI that validates per-recipe ULP tolerance, Augur drift detection. What's missing is the orchestration: a declarative per-output-tolerance budget that drives per-op precision selection.

### 10.2 The user-facing API

```python
with cr.precision_budget(relative_tol=1e-3, reference="fp64_strict"):
    trainer = cr.Trainer(model, recipe="auto")
    for x, y in loader:
        trainer.step(x, y)
```

The user picks an output tolerance (relative or absolute, per operator class or global). Crucible figures out per-op precision allocation that minimizes total cycles within budget.

### 10.3 The pipeline

**Step 1 — Sensitivity profiling.** For a representative sample batch:
1. Run reference plan (CPU `BITEXACT_STRICT` or FP64) to capture per-op output tensors.
2. Run N candidate-precision variants in parallel on fleet GPUs (turbolaunch, §17). Typical N = 8: `{FP32, BF16, FP16/FP32-acc, FP8-E4M3/FP32-acc, FP8-E5M2, FP4-MX, INT8, INT4}`.
3. For each (op, variant), compute `ΔE_{op,variant} = ‖ref − variant‖_relative`.
4. Estimate per-op downstream amplification via one Hutchinson Jacobian probe (one forward + one reverse-mode-AD pass with random vector). `amp_i ≈ ‖∂output / ∂op_i‖_operator`.
5. Materialize sensitivity matrix `S[op, precision] = ΔE × amp`.

**Step 2 — Budget allocation as integer programming.**

```
minimize:    Σ_op cycles[op, precision[op]]
subject to:  Σ_op S[op, precision[op]] ≤ user_budget
             precision[op] ∈ available_recipes_for_op
```

Linear-integer program with ~1000 binary variables for a typical 24-layer transformer × 8 candidates. Solves in <1s with CLP/CBC. Output: per-op recipe choice that minimizes total cycles within tolerance budget.

**Step 3 — Materialize.** Emit a DAG branch with per-op recipes committed. Forge Phase E.RecipeSelect reads the allocation rather than the global default. Per-op recipe lives in the KernelNode's `recipe` field (already specified, FORGE.md §18.2). Atomic swap at iteration boundary.

**Step 4 — Augur drift detection.** The Sharp Matrix Empirical Bernstein scalar (§7.2 scalar 1) samples actual output deviation against the reference at low rate. Sustained deviation >budget × 1.5 triggers re-run of the calibrator with the updated activation distribution; the LP re-solves and the allocation reshuffles. This handles distribution shift.

### 10.4 Why no other framework can build this

Standard ML frameworks lack four prerequisites Crucible has built:

1. **Portable IR with pinned numerics** — without `NumericalRecipe`, swapping per-op precision requires recompilation of the whole graph.
2. **MAP-Elites per kernel × precision × shape** — without per-cell archives, switching precision triggers full re-search.
3. **ExecutionPlan with PatchPoints** — without runtime-mutable plan values, per-op precision alternates require capturing distinct CUDA Graphs.
4. **Cross-vendor CI per recipe** — without tolerance enforcement at recipe level, the LP allocation has no validated bound.

All four are already in Crucible. The calibrator is the orchestration that weaves them into a product.

### 10.5 Composition with `Graded<>`

The budget is a `Budgeted<{LatencyBudget, EnergyBudget, BitsBudget, ToleranceLattice}, T>` from §2.4. The calibrator's LP becomes a constraint on the grade: every operation produces a value of grade ≤ user_budget, and the LP picks the precision recipe that achieves it cheapest.

### 10.6 What it buys

For a typical mixed-precision training workload, the calibrator produces 2-5× speedup over uniform-FP16, with less precision loss than uniform-FP8. The win comes from heterogeneity: some layers (attention LSE accumulation, layer norm reductions) need FP32; most others tolerate FP8 or FP4. A static allowlist either pessimizes most layers or breaks the sensitive few. The LP gets it right per-op, per-batch-distribution, validated.

### 10.7 Cost

Engineering cost: ~8-12 KLoC for the calibrator + sensitivity profiler + LP allocator + DAG-branch materializer + Augur drift hook. The biggest single feature differentiator Crucible could ship.

## 11. Sinkhorn-OT backend for Forge Phase K — placement as optimal transport

### 11.1 The reframing

Forge Phase K (FORGE.md §25.6) currently solves placement via Z3 enumeration over (TP, DP, PP, EP, CP) factor sets with topology-aware constraints. The Z3 invocation is the bottleneck for fleets ≥64 nodes, where the partition search grows past the tractable frontier.

The placement problem is fundamentally an **optimal-transport** problem: distribute work-mass (per-op FLOPs) to capacity-mass (per-Relay measured throughput) minimizing transport cost (latency + bandwidth + energy). Sinkhorn-Knopp solves entropic-regularized OT in essentially linear time per iteration.

The 2025 result that makes this deployable: He, *Phase Transition of the Sinkhorn-Knopp Algorithm* ([arXiv:2507.09711](https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.09711)) proves a sharp γ = 1/2 density threshold in the input matrix. Above γ, Sinkhorn converges in O(log n − log ε) iterations; below, Ω(√n/ε). For Crucible's fleet sizes (n ≤ 1024) and topology matrices that are dense (every Relay-pair has measurable bandwidth), the system is comfortably above γ — sub-millisecond convergence to ε = 1e-4 for n = 128, ~10ms for n = 1024.

### 11.2 The construction

```cpp
namespace forge::phase_k {
    // Cost matrix: distance(op, relay) accounting for topology + capability + role
    using CostMatrix = Matrix<double>;

    // Placement = doubly-stochastic assignment of work to capacity
    using Placement = Matrix<double>;

    Placement sinkhorn_place(
        fx::Bg,
        Span<const FLOPs>     work_mass,      // per-op compute requirement
        Span<const TFlops>    capacity,        // per-Relay measured throughput from Meridian
        const TopologyMatrix& topology,        // pairwise latency / bandwidth from Meridian probes
        SiliconRoleBitmap     work_roles,      // per-op required silicon role
        SiliconRoleBitmap     relay_roles,     // per-Relay available silicon role
        double                eta);            // entropic regularization (lower → sharper, more iterations)
}
```

The cost matrix integrates topology, silicon-role compatibility, and any other Phase K constraint. The output is a doubly-stochastic placement. Heavy-tailed work — large sessions, prefill-heavy mixes — naturally lands on capacity-rich Relays.

### 11.3 Heterogeneous fleets via UOT-TV

Real fleets have mismatched mass: total fleet capacity ≠ total model work. Classical OT requires mass conservation and breaks. **Unbalanced Optimal Total Variation Transport** ([NeurIPS 2025 OpenReview UuhoYEaUhT](https://openreview.net/forum?id=UuhoYEaUhT)) generalizes Sinkhorn to handle this: the objective becomes ∫c(x,y)dπ + λ·TV(supply − marginal). The λ-penalty IS the energy-vs-latency knob — large λ matches every op exactly (inflexible but predictable), small λ permits some work to be deferred (elastic batching, gradient accumulation). UOT-TV solves at the same Sinkhorn complexity.

The UOT-TV penalty parameter becomes a MAP-Elites behavior axis: `mass_balance_slack ∈ {0, 0.05, 0.1, 0.25}`.

### 11.4 Reference curve from McKean-Vlasov hydrodynamic limit

The 2024-2025 work on randomized load-balancing (Aghajani-Ramanan, [arXiv:2402.18143](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.18143)) gives a closed-form stationary queue-length CDF for JSQ(d) under heavy traffic. Augur uses this as a sanity-check reference curve — measured fleet behavior should track the McKean-Vlasov stationary distribution within a known band; deviations >2× indicate mesh misbehavior.

### 11.5 What it buys

Phase K compile time on 64-node fleets drops by an order of magnitude (Sinkhorn at 10ms versus Z3 at hundreds of ms). The placement quality is provably optimal under the entropic relaxation, and the UOT-TV extension handles heterogeneous fleets without ad-hoc weighting. The reference curve from hydrodynamic limits gives Augur a calibrated band to flag mesh anomalies.

## 13. Legion-style automatic iteration-trace memoization

### 13.1 The pattern

**Yadav et al., *Automatic Tracing in Task-Based Runtime Systems*** (ASPLOS 2025) plus *On the Duality of Task and Actor Programming Models* ([arXiv:2508.16522](https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.16522)) demonstrate that detecting repeatedly-executed subgraphs and replaying them as actor-style DAGs collapses the 7× overhead gap between task-based runtimes (Legion, Realm) and actor-based ones (Charm++, MPI), at 3.3–7.1× smaller task granularity. Validated at exascale on Perlmutter and Frontier.

### 13.2 The Crucible integration

The pattern fits Crucible's existing infrastructure exactly. Forge Phase K already does a static partition search via Z3. The Legion insight: do an analogous *dynamic* memoization on warm iterations. A Keeper-side trace cache keyed on `merkle_hash(root_region)` hits when iteration N's ExecutionPlan equals iteration N-1's, and the runtime short-circuits Forge Phases A–E for that iteration.

```cpp
class Keeper {
    // Memoization cache for warm iterations
    Map<MerkleHash, ExecutionPlan*> iteration_trace_cache;

    Future<StepResult> execute_iteration(Region const& root) {
        auto h = root.merkle_hash;
        if (auto plan = iteration_trace_cache.lookup(h)) {
            return submit_plan(plan);  // skip Forge Phases A–E entirely
        }
        auto plan = forge_compile(root);
        iteration_trace_cache.insert(h, plan);
        return submit_plan(plan);
    }
};
```

Crucible already has content-hashed RegionNodes. The trace cache IS Cipher's L1 IR002 tier — what's new is the Keeper-side cache that recognizes when the *whole* iteration is a cache hit, not just sub-regions.

## 14. MDM-OC reversible orthogonal-delta weight merging

### 14.1 The construction

**Modular Delta Merging with Orthogonal Constraints** ([arXiv:2507.20997](https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.20997)) projects task deltas Δ_i = θ_i − θ_base into mutually-orthogonal subspaces P_i(Δ_i) such that ⟨P_i(Δ_i), P_j(Δ_j)⟩ = 0 for i ≠ j. Merge becomes Σ P_i(Δ_i), trivially commutative, associative, idempotent — orthogonality kills cross-terms. Crucially, the merge is **reversible**: subtracting a delta restores the prior state. Classical CRDTs forbid subtraction; MDM-OC requires it.

### 14.2 The Crucible primitive

Once `Graded<>` from §2 lands, MDM-OC becomes a Tagged instance:

```cpp
template <auto Axis>
struct OrthogonalDeltaProvenance {};

template <auto Axis, typename T>
using OrthogonalDelta = Graded<RelativeMonad, OrthogonalProvenance<Axis>, T>;

// A Relay writing partial parameter update for axis (TP=3, DP=7):
auto delta = OrthogonalDelta<Axis::TP3 ⊕ Axis::DP7, WeightShard>::inject(local_update);

// Cipher merges by lattice-join of disjoint tag sets
warm_tier.merge(delta);  // commutative + associative + idempotent + reversible
```

The Khatri-Rao random projection from §6 provides the cheap orthogonalization basis when needed (factored projections cost O(n√d) instead of O(nd)).

### 14.3 What it buys

- **GDPR-style weight retraction.** A user requests their data be removed from a model. Subtract the relevant deltas; the model is restored to a state that never saw that data. No fine-tune-from-checkpoint, no expensive reset.
- **5D-axis composition.** Updates touching cells addressed by multiple parallel axes (TP × DP overlap on a parameter) compose cleanly via the orthogonality kill.
- **Async safety.** Order of arrival doesn't matter for the merge; combined with ordering-replay, async update streams are bit-exact reproducible.

### 14.4 Cost

Engineering cost: ~2-3K LoC for the orthogonal projection / merge / unmerge logic plus an integration with Cipher's warm tier. The orthogonalization basis can be shared across writers for efficiency.

## 15. `TimelineSlice<[k, k+m]>` — time-range-scoped views

### 15.1 The missing scope

Crucible has whole-object scope primitives: `Linear<T>` (all-or-nothing ownership), `BranchNode` (DAG-level), `ScopedView<T>` (lifetime-scoped). What's missing is a scope shape for **time-range subslices** of a session's history.

Use cases that demand this scope:
- **RefreshKV / LAQ** — re-score importance on tokens [k, k+m] without regenerating, while preserving token order and continuing decode forward.
- **TTT (Test-Time Training) inner loop backward** — the inner-loop tape lives within a window of recent activations; mutations on that window must not leak into prior or later windows.
- **Partial rollback for speculative agentic execution** — accept some prefix, roll back the rest.
- **Lookahead Q-cache rescoring** — recompute Q for a range without invalidating K, V.

### 15.2 The construction

```cpp
template <typename Session, auto Start, auto End>
class TimelineSlice {
    // Range-scoped view over Session's token history
    ScopedView<AuxTensor>  mutable_aux();        // can rescore, reflag
    ScopedView<const KV>   readonly_kv();         // cannot mutate underlying KV
    // Linear-typed: exactly one slice per range at a time
    // Composes with ChainEdge: next decode plan waits on this slice's completion
};
```

The slice is `Linear` over the range — exactly one slice per range at a time, enforced at the type level. Mutations on auxiliary tensors are typed as `Refined<P, T>` where P captures "the range was the only writer". The KV cache itself remains read-only through the slice (the view is on auxiliary state, not on the cache).

### 15.3 Composition

`TimelineSlice` composes with `Refined ⊂ Graded` from §2 (the predicate "single-writer-on-range" is a refinement) and with `Permission<Tag>` from CSL (the slice is the permission token for the range). The result is a scoped, type-checked, exclusively-owned view that the type system ensures cannot leak.

### 15.4 Cost

Engineering cost: ~1-2K LoC. The implementation reuses `ScopedView` machinery; the new contribution is the range-scope discipline.

### 15.5 Related work

The pattern echoes database multi-version concurrency control with partial-range locks — `LOCK TABLE ... FOR SHARE PARTITION (rowid_a, rowid_b)`. Functional-programming literature on lenses (over-ranges) is the closest formal analog.

## 16. `SessionOpaqueState<T, Lifetime>` and `SchedulerDirective` — the inferlet pattern

### 16.1 The recurring gap

Across the 7-agent ML-systems survey and the 10-agent adjacent-fields survey, four independent agents flagged variants of the same primitive: **typed runtime state that flows with a session, is content-addressable, optionally Cipher-persistable, and supports a coroutine-style return channel back to the scheduler**.

- Serving (Pie SOSP'25): inferlets — user-space coroutines that read logits/hidden/attention between forward passes and return scheduler directives.
- Distributed training (Decoupled DiLoCo): staleness tag + vector clock per gradient event, treated as session-scoped state.
- Numerical (Kitty online recipe selector): mid-decode precision boost based on observed activation distributions.
- Compiler (CUDA Agent / Kevin): state across mutations for learned-policy kernel search.

All four patterns want the same shape: typed state, scoped to a session/program/fleet lifetime, content-addressed for replay safety, with a structured return-to-scheduler channel.

### 16.2 The construction

```cpp
enum class LifetimeKind : uint8_t {
    PER_REQUEST,    // destroyed on session close
    PER_PROGRAM,    // shared across sibling sessions (tool cache, prefix cache)
    PER_FLEET,      // Raft-replicated (calibration artifacts)
};

template <typename T, LifetimeKind L>
class SessionOpaqueState {
    // Typed, content-addressable, optionally Cipher-persistable
    constexpr T const& peek() const;
    void mutate(std::function<T(T)> f);  // immutable update with content-hash recomputation
    ContentHash hash() const;
};

enum class SchedulerDirective : uint8_t {
    YIELD,        // pause this session, schedule sibling
    PAUSE,        // hold until external signal
    ABORT,        // terminate session, free resources
    FORK,         // create sibling session for speculative decode / beam search
    PERSIST_KV,   // checkpoint KV cache to warm tier
    EMIT_DRAFT,   // emit speculative-decode draft tokens
    PATCH_PLAN,   // request runtime patch of the active ExecutionPlan
};
```

A user-defined inferlet receives session state plus the latest forward-pass output, returns either updated state or a scheduler directive. The Keeper interprets the directive and dispatches accordingly.

```cpp
template <typename UserState>
struct InferletResult {
    std::optional<SchedulerDirective> directive;
    std::optional<UserState> next_state;
    std::optional<std::vector<TokenId>> emit_tokens;
};

template <typename UserState>
using Inferlet = std::function<
    InferletResult<UserState>(UserState const&, ForwardOutput const&)
>;
```

### 16.3 Use cases unlocked

- **PDA stack for grammar-constrained decoding** (XGrammar, Pre3): inferlet maintains a parser stack as `SessionOpaqueState<PdaState, PER_REQUEST>`; returns `EMIT_TOKEN` only for tokens compatible with the current parser state.
- **Tool-call cache** (agentic LLM): `SessionOpaqueState<ToolCacheKeys, PER_PROGRAM>` shares cached tool outputs across sibling sessions.
- **Per-session KV TTL metadata** (Continuum): inferlet inspects KV access pattern, returns `PERSIST_KV` when tail tokens are unlikely to be reaccessed.
- **Online precision boost** (Kitty): inferlet observes activation distributions, returns `PATCH_PLAN` to upgrade FP8→FP16 mid-decode.
- **Speculative tool execution**: inferlet returns `FORK` to run a tool call in parallel while the main session continues; merge or discard based on user acceptance.

### 16.4 Composition with `Graded<>`

Lifetime kind is a grade: `PER_REQUEST < PER_PROGRAM < PER_FLEET`. `SessionOpaqueState<T, L>` is `Graded<LifetimeLattice, T>` with a counit that downgrades a longer lifetime to a shorter one (a fleet-scoped value can be read as program-scoped, but not vice versa).

### 16.5 Cited papers

- *Pie: An Inferlet Operating System* (SOSP 2025). The OS-level inspiration for the inferlet pattern.
- vLLM Continuum (KV TTL).
- XGrammar / Pre3 (grammar-constrained decoding).

## 17. Turbolaunch — calibration as a first-class GPU workload

### 17.1 The current bottleneck

Meridian's calibration today (MIMIC.md §22) runs ~150-200 microbenchmarks per SM per chip target sequentially. Each microbench is a CUDA kernel launch, a CUPTI counter read, and a CPU-side analysis. At standard SDK throughput, a fresh chip target takes hours to days to calibrate to ~95% accuracy. A fleet of 8 chip variants takes a week of wall time to calibrate from scratch.

This is the wrong order of magnitude for the Crucible model. The fleet should calibrate in seconds to minutes.

### 17.2 The construction

Pack the entire calibration suite into one pushbuffer per chip:

```cpp
namespace mimic::calibrate {
    // Per-chip calibration suite as a single ExecutionPlan
    ExecutionPlan compile_calibration_plan(
        fx::Init,
        ChipId chip,
        Span<const Microbench> benches);

    // Each chip launches its own pushbuffer simultaneously
    Future<CalibrationResults> launch_fleet_wide(
        fx::Bg,
        Canopy const& canopy,
        ExecutionPlan const& plan);
}
```

Each microbench becomes a QMD in the calibration plan. CUPTI-equivalent counter reads become pushbuffer-embedded methods (NVIDIA `PMASS_READ` + semaphore write; AMD `RELEASE_MEM` with ATOMIC payload). One doorbell write per chip launches the entire suite. Per-chip wall-clock = slowest microbench's runtime, dominated by device execution rather than driver overhead.

Fleet-wide: Canopy Raft-commits "calibration epoch E"; every Keeper issues its pushbuffer at the same instant. 8 chips calibrate in parallel, then 64, then 1024.

### 17.3 What it buys

- **Fresh chip target calibrated to 95% in <5 seconds** instead of hours.
- **Cipher-cached per-chip plans** — second-time calibration on the same chip family is a doorbell write.
- **MAP-Elites real-hardware validation in the inner loop** — turbolaunch makes hardware-validation cheap enough that HYBRID search mode (FORGE.md §22.8) becomes the default rather than a debug-only mode.
- **Precision-budget calibrator (§10) runs in <2s** per fleet — usable for online distribution-shift adaptation.
- **Augur drift detection** — every sampled kernel gets full hardware counters at ~0 additional cost, so the six-scalar dashboard from §7 runs at higher rate without budget impact.

### 17.4 Cost

Engineering cost: ~3-5K LoC, mostly in the per-vendor runtime layer wiring CUPTI-equivalent counter reads into pushbuffer method sequences. The rest reuses ExecutionPlan machinery.

### 17.5 Related references

- The pattern is original to Crucible — driven by the design pressure that calibration must be cheap enough to use online, not just at install time.

## 18. Prism parallelization axioms + cross-instance fusion rewriter

### 18.1 The Prism axioms

Wu, Jiang, Padon, Jia (2026), *Prism: Symbolic Superoptimization of Tensor Programs* ([arXiv:2604.15272](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.15272)) gives a ~70-axiom rewrite library for tensor programs at the parallelization level: four parallelized-matmul forms (reduction, row-partition, col-partition, leading-dim), eight compound-parallelized-sum forms, plus commutativity/cancellation rules over the four parallelization operators (part / comb / red / repl).

These are pure algebraic rewrites that catch partition-refactor opportunities Crucible's current Phase C rewriter misses. They land cleanly in Forge Phase C.1 as additional rewrite rules (~500 LoC of rules + tests). Each rule carries an `fp_associative: bool` flag: under `BITEXACT_TC`/`BITEXACT_STRICT`, ULP-level re-association is forbidden; the rules are gated off and the recipe tier dictates strict reduction order.

### 18.2 Cross-instance fusion

The 7-agent ML-systems survey identified low-utilization matmul (decode attention with M=1, multi-session scattered work) as a frequently-missed opportunity. The remedy is a **structural rewriter** that detects low-intensity matmul (M×N×K below the per-chip saturation threshold) and searches for fusion partners that share an output or input dimension:

- Multiple sessions' decode Q·K^T per step → `RAGGED_ATTN` with `cu_seqlens`
- MoE per-expert GEMM with variable per-expert token counts → `MOE_ROUTE` + grouped GEMM
- Per-head attention on GQA/MQA with shared K, V → grouped batched launch
- Multi-Q speculative decoding verify → batch_size=N verify
- Multi-LoRA adapters on same base → simultaneous multi-output GEMM

Each pattern compiles to an existing Mimic kernel template. The new contribution is the **rewriter** that detects fusion partners and emits the right ragged/grouped/swarm template.

### 18.3 K-cooperative CTA-swarm for matvec

For the M=1 / batch=1 matvec case (single-token decode), spawn N_SM CTAs with each computing a tile of the output. Warps within each CTA cooperate on loading K-slices; output reduce via smem tree. Turns a single-digit-MFU matvec into 40-60% MFU on decode. Lands as a new Mimic template in `mimic/<vendor>/templates/`.

## 19. Arithmetic-intensity-aware co-scheduling via green contexts

### 19.1 The current state

Crucible already supports green contexts (CRUCIBLE.md §14.9): NVIDIA Hopper+'s hardware SM-partitioning that allocates compute / dispatch / combine / scheduler contexts to disjoint SM subsets. The default partition is workload-class-aware (dense transformer vs. MoE vs. context-parallel) but **statically configured**.

### 19.2 The extension

Classify each kernel by its bottleneck — FLOP-bound (tensor-core saturated), BW-bound (HBM-saturated), LAT-bound (latency-tail), SYNC-bound (mbarrier-dominated) — using Mimic's profile outputs. Co-schedule complementary classes on disjoint green contexts: a FLOP-bound kernel's hot resource is tensor cores; a BW-bound kernel's hot resource is L2/HBM; running them concurrently on disjoint SMs *increases* aggregate throughput because they don't fight for the same bottleneck.

```cpp
struct ArithmeticIntensity {
    BottleneckClass primary;  // FLOP | BW | LAT | SYNC
    double          flops_per_byte;
    double          bytes_per_cycle;
};

struct ExecutionAttrs {
    // existing fields
    SiliconRole       silicon_role;
    ArithmeticIntensity intensity;
    GreenContextId    preferred_green_context;  // co-scheduling hint
};
```

Phase I.StreamAssign pairs FLOP+BW classes on different green contexts; same-class pairs serialize. L2 persisting policies (CUDA `cudaAccessPolicyWindow`) pin the BW-class kernel's working set so it doesn't evict the FLOP-class kernel's tensor-core data.

Augur's MFU signal feeds dynamic re-provisioning of green-context partitions: if compute context is under-utilized while dispatch context is saturated, propose a re-split at next iteration boundary (CRUCIBLE.md §14.9.6 already mentions this as a future extension).

### 19.3 Cost

Engineering cost: ~4-6K LoC in Phase I.StreamAssign + arithmetic-intensity classification (mostly already in Mimic insights) + green-context re-provisioning machinery. Estimated MFU improvement: +10% aggregate on workloads with mixed-intensity kernels (consistent with DeepSeek-V3 public numbers for their inference stack).

## 20. Refactor targets — Mealy unification, BI memory-plan verification

### 20.1 Effectful Mealy machines unifying Session + ExecutionPlan + ChainEdge

**Bonchi, Di Lavore, Román, *Effectful Mealy Machines: Bisimulation and Trace*** (LICS 2025 / CALCO 2025, [LIPIcs page](https://drops.dagstuhl.de/storage/00lipics/lipics-vol342-calco2025/html/LIPIcs.CALCO.2025.1/LIPIcs.CALCO.2025.1.html)) present a uniform algebraic framework for state machines with output effects. Three of Crucible's primitives are visibly the same shape:

- `Session<Send<T, Recv<U, End>>>` — input/output with a state index and protocol residue effect.
- `ExecutionPlan + PatchPoint` — input patches → output doorbell with a device-command-buffer effect.
- `ChainEdge` semaphore — causal stream between plans with a fence-counter effect.

All three are instances of `Mealy<E, In, Out>` with different E. The LICS 2025 theorem says bisimilarity for all three reduces to free-uniform-feedback equivalence — i.e., the *same compiler pass* proves replay-determinism for a session, a plan-chain, and an MPST handshake. Currently Crucible has three separate determinism-proof paths.

Refactoring to `Mealy<E, In, Out>` plus three E specializations cuts ~2K LoC from `Session*.h` + `PlanComposer` + `ChainEdge.h` and gives one bisimilarity-test entry point. The refactor sits on top of the §2 `Graded<>` work.

### 20.2 Bunched implications on memory plans

**Gu, Gheorghiu, Pym, *Proof-theoretic Semantics for BI*** (Studia Logica 2025) and Liu et al. *Quantified Underapproximation via Labeled Bunches* (OOPSLA2 2025) give the formal semantics for separation logic with quantitative labels. Memory plans already exhibit the structure: `slot[i] *_plan slot[j]` when their `[birth, death]` intervals are disjoint OR their offsets don't overlap is pure BI separating conjunction `*`.

Today this invariant is established by the sweep-line planner and **trusted at runtime** — there's no compile-time witness. Encoding `MemoryPlan` as a `consteval` BI-term over `Slot<id, offset, size, [birth, death]>` atoms makes the planner emit a proof term that the compiler checks via `static_assert(bi_sep<S1, S2>)`. The MemSafe-at-plan-level invariant from CRUCIBLE.md §L3 becomes a compile-time witness.

Labeled bunches additionally label each region with its `Permission<Tag>` — unifying CSL separation with the memory planner. The `verify` preset (CMake §I) discharges BI proof obligations to Z3.

### 20.3 Reach capabilities for container types

**System Capless**, *What's in the Box* ([ACM DL 3763112](https://dl.acm.org/doi/abs/10.1145/3763112)) introduces `rcap x` ("reach capability of x") to name what's inside a generic container without enumerating it. This solves the long-standing `Linear<Container<Linear<FD>>>` expressibility gap in Crucible: today there's no way to say "a `Vec<Linear<FD>>` whose elements' capabilities reach into capture-set c". A phantom template `Reach<c, T>` makes per-element ownership visible to the type checker without unrolling.

Useful for the TraceRing/MetaLog refactors in Phase 2a where permission-tagged containers currently require hand-audit.

## 21. Research horizon

These are the longer-term commitments — well-defined, theorem-backed, but bounded engineering effort that doesn't fit Phase 2a/2b. Each is high-value and well-scoped; none is on the critical path.

### 21.1 E-graph engine in Forge Phase C with SmoothE-style differentiable extraction

**SmoothE: Differentiable E-Graph Extraction** (ASPLOS 2025 Best Paper, [ACM DL 3707262](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3669940.3707262)) replaces the ILP in `extract_best(egraph, cost)` with a continuous probabilistic optimization solved by gradient descent on GPU. Supports non-linear cost functions (which ILP cannot), 8-37× faster. Crucible's per-vendor cost — predicted_cycles + cross-vendor ULP drift + compiled-bytes — is a non-linear cost function; ILP-based extractors are a non-starter.

Adding an e-graph engine to Forge Phase C unlocks bi-directional rewriting (currently Crucible's peephole is uni-directional), contextual axioms gated on BranchNode arms ([SAS 2025 contextual e-graphs](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-032-07106-4_3)), and quotient types on content hashes (e-classes naturally content-addressed). Engineering cost: ~10-15K LoC, including porting an `egg`-style engine to C++26 or FFI to Rust.

### 21.2 SM-level task graph IR

**Mirage MPK** (CMU Catalyst, arXiv:2512.22219) and **ThunderKittens PGL** (Hazy Research 2025-09) demonstrate decentralized SM-level scheduling across 132 SMs with per-task queues fed by data-dependent edges. Crucible's dynamic on-GPU scheduler (CRUCIBLE.md §3.9) uses a centralized work queue; MPK/PGL use decentralized queues per SM with explicit cross-SM dependency edges.

Adding an IR tier between KernelKind and PushBuffer modeling per-SM tasks plus a `SymmetricGlobalLayout` tensor attribute for NVLink-addressable multi-GPU mega-kernels enables the megakernel patterns ThunderKittens validated for Llama-1B inference and DeepEP Mega-MoE. Engineering cost: ~15-20K LoC. Phase 4+ commitment, not blocking v1.

### 21.3 Task sheaves with cohomological obstruction

**Felber, Hummes Flores, Rincon Galeana, *A Sheaf-Theoretic Characterization of Tasks in Distributed Systems*** (SIROCCO 2025, [arXiv:2503.02556](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.02556)) define a task sheaf `F : Cov(X)^op → Set` on the poset of execution cuts. Global sections `H^0(X, F)` = terminating distributed solutions. Non-trivial `H^1(X, F)` = cohomological obstruction — an impossibility certificate, computable linear-algebraically.

Crucible currently has zero machinery for proving that a proposed reshard/replan is *feasible* across the Canopy before attempting it. Meridian solves a Z3 5D partition; Augur monitors drift; neither can say "this plan has no global section" *before* the Keeper commits the atomic swap. A task sheaf over `(Relay, view-at-iteration-k)` cells with stalks = shard assignments and restriction maps = permitted handoffs gives a pre-commit obstruction check: compute δ on candidate section, non-zero row = a Relay pair that cannot agree on the reshard.

Sits as a new layer between L13 (Distribution) and L15 (Meridian+Augur). Engineering cost: ~5-8K LoC plus the cohomology computation library. Phase 5+ commitment.

### 21.4 Synthetic MPST + AMP + PReST refactor of the 12-layer session stack

Three 2025-2026 session-type results, taken together, restructure Crucible's 12-layer MPST stack:

- **Castro-Perez, Ferreira, Jongmans, *Synthetic MPST*** (POPL 2026, [arXiv:2511.22692](https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.22692)) type-checks each process directly against a global LTS — no local projection, no merge, no intermediate local types. Effectively deletes the Honda → HYC projection chain (Crucible layers S1-S2).
- **Stutz & D'Osualdo, *AMP: Automata-theoretic Basis for Multiparty Protocols*** (ESOP 2025, [arXiv:2501.16977](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.16977)) replace syntactic global types with Protocol State Machines (PSMs) and Communicating State Machines (CSMs), strictly more expressive than any global-type grammar. New foundation layer S0' under the existing stack.
- **Fu, Das, Gaboardi, *Probabilistic Refinement Session Types (PReST)*** (PLDI 2025, [ACM DL 3729317](https://dl.acm.org/doi/pdf/10.1145/3729317)) add SMT-checkable refinement predicates over arithmetic, including symbolic probabilities. Subsumes the SY19 parametric φ tier (S7) and extends it.

Together they could collapse layers S2 + S7 + S8 into a unified refined/synthetic tier. Engineering cost: ~15-25K LoC over Phase 5+. Strategic refactor target rather than blocking v1.

### 21.5 Symbolic-parallel-mapping IR tier

Prism's symbolic mapping variables `m_{T,d,p} ∈ {0,1}` (which data dim of which tensor is partitioned along which parallelization dim) live one level below IR002 — neither dense IR002 nor per-vendor IR003* expresses partition mappings as first-class symbolic variables. A new tier between IR002 and IR003 would carry `(dim_var → parallel_axis)` assignments as first-class terms.

This unlocks Forge Phase K pruning *before* Z3 by exhaustively enumerating partition structures and discarding incompatible ones via Prism's symbolic-dimension matching (§3.2 of the Prism paper). Engineering cost: ~8-12K LoC. Phase 5+ research bet.

### 21.6 `WASSERSTEIN_OPTIMAL` recipe tier

The thermodynamic Wasserstein-2 dissipation bound (§7.2 scalar 2) proves Σ ≥ W₂² / (2·D·τ). A new NumericalRecipe tier `WASSERSTEIN_OPTIMAL` chooses a slower, lower-dissipation protocol — trades τ for Σ at the recipe level. Useful for energy-constrained deployments (battery-powered inference, datacenter peak-shaving).

Engineering cost: ~2-3K LoC (a new tier in the registry plus per-vendor realization). Phase 6 commitment.

## 22. What we explicitly do NOT adopt, and why

The survey identified ~50 candidate primitives. Many do not survive the bit-exact replay-determinism axiom or the "expressible as composition of existing" test. Documenting the rejections is as important as documenting the adoptions — they prevent future cycles re-litigating the same calls.

### 22.1 Physics analogies that do not transfer

**Kuramoto FedAvg** ([arXiv:2505.19605](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2505.19605)) — re-labelling of gradient-direction with no new mechanism. Demonstrated only on non-IID image benchmarks; gains over plain FedAvg are small. Crucible's Canopy already has Raft for consensus and SWIM for membership; neither needs an oscillator framing. Matrix-weighted Kuramoto K_c ([arXiv:2603.08352](https://arxiv.org/html/2603.08352)) is mathematically rigorous but only useful as a clock-mesh diagnostic, not a primitive.

**Vicsek correlation length** ([arXiv:2504.00511](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2504.00511)) — pure analogy. Diagnostic for physicists studying bacterial suspensions; no quantitative threshold transfers to engineering.

**TASEP hydrodynamic limits** ([arXiv:2104.04444](https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.04444), [arXiv:2301.04066](https://arxiv.org/abs/2301.04066)) — j = ρ(1−ρ) current-density relation gives a pipeline-parallelism throughput maximum at ρ=1/2, but Crucible already measures this directly via Meridian and tunes PP-depth empirically. Adds no actionable bound.

**Keller-Segel chemotaxis on metric graphs** ([PMC11646970](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11646970/)) — chemotactic routing is strictly worse than explicit shortest-queue probes at Crucible's fleet sizes (n ≤ 1024). Volume-filling overcrowding terms make the dynamics fragile in high-density regimes — a correctness liability, not an asset.

**Turing patterns** ([Sci Rep 2025](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-86854-7)) — spontaneous spatial inhomogeneity is exactly what we don't want in a scheduler. Cite only as a thing to monitor against.

**Phase-ordering coarsening** ([arXiv:2510.15410](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2510.15410)) — power-law convergence to consensus, strictly slower than Raft's bounded-round commits and lacking Byzantine fault tolerance.

**Active matter correlation length** ([arXiv:2504.00511](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2504.00511)) — same family as Vicsek; rejected for the same reason.

### 22.2 Hardware proposals not yet ready

**Extropic / Normal Computing thermodynamic-computing p-bits** ([Extropic DTM](https://extropic.ai/writing/thermodynamic-computing-from-zero-to-one), [arXiv:2510.23972](https://arxiv.org/html/2510.23972)) — 10000× energy-efficiency claim is Fashion-MNIST-scale simulation-calibrated. Hardware not yet at datacenter scale. Monitor; revisit when Z1 ships (early 2026 projected).

### 22.3 Mathematically interesting but operationally vacuous

**Jarzynski/Crooks fluctuation theorems for training trajectories** — mathematically analogous to SGD but no new metric. "Dissipated work" is already the loss curve.

**Maxwell-demon Pareto fronts** ([PRR 7, 023159](https://journals.aps.org/prresearch/abstract/10.1103/PhysRevResearch.7.023159)) — beautiful Langevin-only result; no clean mapping to discrete kernel scheduling.

**Inverse Thermodynamic Uncertainty Relation (TUR)** ([arXiv:2503.14204](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.14204)) — bounds Var(J)/⟨J⟩² ≥ 2/Σ. Cute consistency check; not a primitive. Keep as an Augur sanity assertion if free; don't build infrastructure for it.

### 22.4 Reduces to existing primitives

**Pinned ∞-operads for ExecutionPlan chaining** ([arXiv:2301.01199](https://arxiv.org/abs/2301.01199)) — operadic composition is strictly stronger than what Crucible needs. The Merkle DAG plus effectful Mealy (§20.1) already yields associativity. No new information.

**Katara-style verified-lifting CRDT synthesis** ([PLDI 2025, arXiv:2205.12425](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2205.12425)) — tooling upgrade. Auto-generates merge functions from sequential specs via SMT; useful but not a primitive. Monotonic + AppendOnly + the `verify` preset already provide the substrate.

**δ-vector-clock optimization for pure-op CRDTs** (ECOOP 2025) — only useful as a dependency of MDM-OC (§14). Standalone, HLC + content-hash Merkle chains beat δ-VC at 10K-worker scale.

### 22.5 Breaks bit-exact discipline

**Nezha multi-rail allreduce with reliability-aware rail switching** ([arXiv:2405.17870](https://arxiv.org/html/2405.17870v1)) — cost-feedback load balancing across rails is fundamentally non-deterministic; the chunk-to-rail mapping varies with measured latency. Adopt for ORDERED/UNORDERED tier only; reject for BITEXACT_STRICT.

### 22.6 Already cancelled

**F\*X dependent-type kernel-optimality engine** — explicitly cancelled in the recent CLAUDE.md refactor. The replacement is MAP-Elites + calibrated simulators + cross-vendor CI plus the optional Z3 `verify` preset for residual integer-arithmetic obligations.
